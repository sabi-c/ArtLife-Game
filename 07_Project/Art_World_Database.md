## I. Scandals & Fraud

### 1. Knoedler Gallery Forgery Ring ($80M)
**When**: 1994–2011 | **Where**: New York

The most respected gallery in America — open since 1846 — sold 60+ fake Abstract Expressionist paintings for $80M over 17 years. Chinese artist Pei-Shen Qian painted them in his Queens garage. Dealer Glafira Rosales supplied them to director Ann Freedman, who may or may not have known.

**What makes it wild**: The gallery was so trusted that collectors didn't question provenance. Freedman claimed she was a victim too. The gallery closed in 2011 rather than face trial.

> **🎮 Game event**: A trusted dealer offers you a "lost masterwork" at a suspiciously good price. Accept the deal (risk forgery), demand authentication (costs time + money), or tip off a journalist (burns the relationship, boosts intel).

---

### 2. Wolfgang Beltracchi — The Greatest Forger ($100M+)
**When**: 1970s–2010 | **Where**: Germany

Beltracchi didn't copy existing paintings — he invented "undiscovered" works in famous artists' styles and fabricated their entire provenance histories, including forging old photographs of the paintings hanging in fictional collections. He was only caught when a pigment (titanium white) in a Heinrich Campendonk fake was found not to exist during the artist's lifetime.

**What makes it wild**: He served only 3 years of a 6-year sentence. Now sells paintings under his own name for high prices.

> **🎮 Game event**: A provenance trail you're following leads to suspicious old photographs. Investigation costs $5K and 2 weeks. If you skip it and the work is a forgery, your reputation takes a devastating hit.

---

### 3. Inigo Philbrick — The Art World's Madoff ($86M)
**When**: 2016–2019 | **Where**: London/Miami/Vanuatu

Young, charismatic art dealer sold more than 100% ownership of individual artworks to multiple investors simultaneously. Used art he didn't own as collateral for loans. Falsified documents to inflate values. When it collapsed, he fled to the South Pacific island of Vanuatu. Arrested there in 2020. Sentenced to 7 years.

**What makes it wild**: He was released in March 2024 after serving less than 4 years.

> **🎮 Game event**: An NPC dealer offers fractional ownership in a hot artist's piece. If you buy in, you later discover two other collectors also "own" the same painting. Pursue legal action (expensive), cut your losses, or use the intel as leverage.

---

### 4. The Bouvier Affair ($1B+ in Transactions)
**When**: 2000s–2015 | **Where**: Switzerland/Monaco

Swiss art dealer Yves Bouvier acted as "advisor" to Russian billionaire Dmitry Rybolovlev while secretly buying works himself and flipping them to Rybolovlev at massive markups. Rybolovlev paid $127.5M for a Modigliani that Bouvier had just bought for $93.5M. Over hundreds of transactions, the markup was over $1 billion.

**What makes it wild**: Exposed the total opacity of private art sales. Neither buying nor selling prices are public in most art deals — the entire system runs on trust.

> **🎮 Game event**: Your advisor NPC has been buying works "on your behalf" — but you discover they've been pocketing a hidden markup. Confront them (lose the relationship), fire them quietly, or use the knowledge to negotiate better future deals.

---

### 5. Lisa Schiff — Art Advisor Fraud ($6.5M)
**When**: 2022–2023 | **Where**: New York

Lisa Schiff, a prominent art advisor with an elite client list, was sued for defrauding clients of approximately $6.5M. She allegedly took money for artwork purchases, then never delivered the work. Some clients paid for pieces that were never acquired.

> **🎮 Game event**: Phone message from a panicked fellow collector — "Did you send money to [advisor]? Because I did and I can't reach them." Do you warn others (reputation boost, but you're now involved), go directly to the advisor, or stay silent?

---

### 6. Mary Boone — Gallery Tax Fraud
**When**: 2018 | **Where**: New York

Mary Boone, a legendary 1980s art dealer who helped launch Jean-Michel Basquiat, Julian Schnabel, and David Salle, went to prison for tax fraud. She failed to report over $3.5M in income and used client funds for personal expenses.

> **🎮 Game event**: A legendary dealer approaches you about a deal — but rumors swirl about their finances. The price is incredible. Do you take the risk?

---

### 7. Han van Meegeren — The Vermeer Forger Who Fooled the Nazis
**When**: 1937–1945 | **Where**: The Netherlands

Van Meegeren created fake Vermeers so convincing that Hermann Göring bought one. After WWII, he was charged with selling Dutch cultural heritage to the Nazis — he confessed to the forgeries to save himself from the more serious charge. He became a folk hero.

> **🎮 Game event**: Historical flavor text / Intel tab item about the thin line between genius and fraud.

---

### 8. Orlando Museum of Art — Fake Basquiats
**When**: 2022 | **Where**: Florida

The museum exhibited 25 works attributed to Basquiat that the FBI later determined were forgeries. How were they caught? A modern-era FedEx logo was visible on the back of one painting's cardboard. The museum's director was fired.

> **🎮 Game event**: A museum invites you to a "newly discovered" exhibition. Your Intel score determines whether you notice something is off.

---

### 9. Sotheby's & Christie's Commission Fixing
**When**: 2000 | **Where**: New York/London

The two biggest auction houses in the world were caught colluding to fix the commission rates they charged sellers. Sotheby's chairman Alfred Taubman went to prison. Christie's got immunity for cooperating. The scandal revealed how cozy the supposedly competitive auction world really was.

> **🎮 Game event**: Both major auction houses offer you the same suspiciously identical commission rate. You can accept (good deal), investigate (intel boost), or leverage one against the other.

---

### 10. The Salvator Mundi Authenticity War
**When**: 2017–Present | **Where**: Global

Sold for $450.3M as a Leonardo da Vinci painting. Many scholars believe it's largely by workshop assistants, or may not be Leonardo's at all. It hasn't been publicly displayed since the sale. The buyer is widely believed to be Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman.

> **🎮 Game event**: You own a work whose attribution is publicly challenged. Your portfolio value swings wildly. Do you defend the attribution (costs reputation), sell before the debate worsens, or commission independent scholarship?

## III. Power Players: Dealers & Galleries

### 21. Larry Gagosian — The King
**Founded**: 1978 (LA poster sales → global empire)
**Locations**: 20+ galleries worldwide
**Annual revenue**: $1B+
**Key artists**: Damien Hirst, Jeff Koons, Richard Serra, Cecily Brown + estates of Basquiat, Warhol, Twombly

Started selling posters on the street in LA. Now the most powerful dealer alive. Called "Go-Go" for his relentless hustle. Controls supply by representing both living artists and their estates.

> **🎮 NPC archetype**: The mega-dealer who can make or break your career. Getting his attention is a blue option — requires Reputation 80+.

### 22. David Zwirner — The Strategist
**Founded**: 1993 (SoHo)
**Locations**: NY, LA, London, Paris, Hong Kong
**Key artists**: Yayoi Kusama, Kerry James Marshall, Luc Tuymans, estates of Dan Flavin

Son of prominent dealer Rudolf Zwirner. Known for rigorous curatorial vision and giving platforms to artists before they blow up. Pioneer of online sales and art world publishing.

> **🎮 NPC archetype**: The dealer who respects knowledge. Approaching with high Intel unlocks better deals.

### 23. Hauser & Wirth — The Family Empire
**Founded**: 1992 (Zurich)
**Leaders**: Iwan & Manuela Wirth, Ursula Hauser
**Key artists**: Louise Bourgeois, Philip Guston, Eva Hesse
**Locations**: Zurich, London, NY, LA, Hong Kong, Somerset (rural arts center)

Family-run mega-gallery that tops the art world power lists. Known for turning historic buildings into art spaces and for long-term relationships with artists and estates.

> **🎮 NPC archetype**: The institutional voice. Attending their events opens doors to the museum world.

### 24. Pace Gallery — The Legacy House
**Founded**: 1960 (Boston, by Arne Glimcher)
**Now led by**: Marc Glimcher (son)
**Key artists/estates**: Calder, Rothko, Agnes Martin, Dubuffet

### 26. White Cube — The YBA Factory

**Venue**: Fondation Louis Vuitton (Paris, Frank Gehry building)

**Founded**: Saatchi Collection (early purchases: Sol LeWitt, Donald Judd)
**Famous for**: Discovering and bankrolling the Young British Artists (Damien Hirst, Tracey Emin)

**Focus**: Warhol, Lichtenstein, Basquiat, Koons

## V. Legendary Heists

### 33. Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum Heist ($500M)
**When**: March 18, 1990 | **Where**: Boston

Two men dressed as police officers talked their way past security at 1:24 AM. Stole 13 works in 81 minutes: Vermeer's *The Concert*, Rembrandt's *The Storm on the Sea of Galilee*, 3 Degas works, a Manet, a Flinck. Total value: $500M. **None have ever been recovered.** The museum still displays empty frames where the paintings hung. $10M reward still active.

> **🎮 Game event**: A mysterious figure contacts you about a work "from a private collection" that matches a famous stolen piece. Enormous risk-reward scenario.

---

### 34. The Mona Lisa Theft (1911)
**When**: August 21, 1911 | **Where**: The Louvre, Paris

Italian handyman Vincenzo Peruggia hid in the Louvre overnight, lifted the Mona Lisa off the wall, and walked out with it under his coat. The painting was missing for over 2 years. Peruggia claimed he wanted to return it to Italy. The theft made the Mona Lisa the most famous painting in history — it was relatively obscure before.

> **🎮 Intel tab lore**: "The most famous painting in the world is famous because it was stolen."

---

### 35. The Scream — Stolen Twice
**When**: 1994 and 2004 | **Where**: Oslo, Norway

The 1994 thieves left a note: "Thanks for the poor security." The painting was recovered 3 months later. In 2004, masked gunmen stole another version from the Munch Museum at gunpoint, along with *Madonna*. Both recovered in 2006.

---

### 36. The "Takeaway" Rembrandt
*Portrait of Jacob de Gheyn III* — stolen 4 times since 1966. Guinness World Record for most stolen painting. Found under a park bench, on a luggage rack, in a taxi, and behind a church. Each time, recovered. Nobody can keep it.

## VII. Market Manipulation Tactics

These are the real tactics used to manipulate the art market — and they're game mechanics waiting to happen:

### Shill Bidding
Confederates place fake bids to drive up auction prices. The real buyer ends up paying more than they should.

> **🎮 Mechanic**: At auctions, some bids are fake. High Intel score lets you detect shills and know when to stop bidding.

### Chandelier Bidding
Auctioneers take bids "off the chandelier" — fabricating bids from the room to create a sense of competition.

> **🎮 Mechanic**: Auction events include pressure from phantom bidders. More experienced players learn to read the room.

### Gallery Buybacks
Galleries buy their own artists' work at auction to prevent prices from falling. Called "protecting the market."

> **🎮 Mechanic**: An artist's price at auction doesn't reflect true demand — a gallery might be propping it up. Intel reveals the truth.

### The Freeport System
Wealthy collectors store art in tax-free zones (Geneva, Luxembourg, Singapore) to avoid import duties and sales tax. Art can be bought, stored, and resold without ever leaving the freeport. Estimated $100B+ in art stored in freeports worldwide.

> **🎮 Late-game mechanic**: Unlock freeport storage. Reduces tax costs but requires upfront investment.

### The Waiting List
Mega-galleries don't sell to just anyone. You have to "qualify" — prove you'll hold the work, not flip it. Galleries maintain blacklists of flippers.

> **🎮 Mechanic**: If you flip works too fast, dealers stop selling to you. Must build Reputation before accessing primary market.

### The Guarantee
Auction houses guarantee a minimum price to sellers. If the work doesn't sell above that price, the auction house buys it themselves. This removes risk for the seller but means some "record prices" are artificially supported.

> **🎮 Mechanic**: When consigning works to auction, you can pay for a guarantee (costs commission) or take the risk.

## X. Auction House Archetypes & Mechanics

To make the "Advanced Auction System" feel real, we map mechanics to these archetypes:

| Archetype | Real-World Equivalent | Primary Auction Mechanic | Game Feel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Blue-Chip House** | Christie's / Sotheby's | **English Auction (Open)** | High heat, fast pace, psychological pressure. "Going once..." |
| **The Distinctive House** | Phillips / Bonhams | **Guaranteed/Fixed Price** | Curated sales. "We have a guarantee on this lot." Less risk, lower reward. |
| **The Estate Liquidator** | Regional Auctioneers | **Sealed Bid** | "Submit your best offer by Friday." Blind strategy. High variance. |
| **The Private Dealer** | Gagosian / Zwirner | **One-Offer** | "I'm offering this to you for $2M. Yes or no? You have 60 seconds." |
| **The Shadow Market** | Freeport Trading | **Double Auction / Bulk** | Buying entire crates/collections sight unseen. High risk. |

> **Cohesion Note**: When designing the "Interactive Auction System" (Roadmap Phase 4), use these archetypes to determine *which* mini-game the player encounters.
